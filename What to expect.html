What to expect ?

El Salvador is small in territory but has one of the biggest hearts in the continent. It has a rich history that is palpable to this day. The population does not forget its hardships and keeps the memory of the heroes and villains alive. The two greatest examples for both sides are the Train Museum which tells the story of the early republic and the Jesuit Museum that tells a tragic chapter of the Salvadorean war in the 80s. Another attraction that you might like is that there are celebrations all the time and everywhere. Depending on the time of the year and what side of the country you are in, you might find a lot of festivals and carnivals going on, especially in the small towns, which are the most likely to live off tourism. On the west side you can encounter Ataco which is the most iconic and visited town of the region, the colonial architecture and the climate generate that feeling of time travel when you see it from the mountains that surround it. The west side is warmer one department of the territory is known as “The Devils Cauldron;” the name is appropriate considering it is so hot and desertic. My personal recommendation is that you learn the art of what a good Salvadorean knows as “puebleando,” this means you plan on visiting towns that are neighboring to big tourist attractions. These towns usually have something unique to them that websites or guides will not tell you about.
